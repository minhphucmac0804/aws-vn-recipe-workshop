This section summarizes how I experienced the program, what kinds of guidance helped me most, and what I think could be improved for future interns.

## Overall evaluation

**1. Working environment**

The learning environment was flexible and supportive, which was important because my project path was not straightforward from the beginning. I spent a large part of the internship balancing broad technical learning, graduation-project work, and later the workshop submission itself, so having room to learn and adjust direction mattered a lot. At the same time, that flexibility also meant I had to develop stronger self-management and clearer project narrowing over time.

**2. Support from mentor / program admin**

The support I received was valuable not because the team directly guided the technical direction of this specific project, but because they were willing to help whenever they could so that I could successfully complete the internship. That support mattered in a broader sense: it made it easier for me to keep moving when I was uncertain, to continue learning while the project direction was still forming, and to stay committed to finishing the work properly.

**3. Relevance of work to academic major**

The internship was related to my academic background, but not in a simple one-to-one way. My stronger starting point was in mathematics, statistics, and machine learning ideas rather than cloud engineering or deployment. Because of that, the project was useful precisely because it forced me to connect familiar ML concepts such as embeddings, retrieval, and evaluation with areas I was much weaker in, such as AWS services, deployment flow, and reviewer-facing system design.

**4. Learning and skill development opportunities**

This internship gave me meaningful growth across several layers at once. On the AWS side, I improved in using Lambda, ECR, DynamoDB, CloudWatch, EventBridge Scheduler, SNS, and IAM-related setup. On the ML side, I developed a more practical understanding of retrieval systems, vector search, embeddings, dataset quality, and the difference between a system that runs and a system that retrieves and answers well. I also improved in documentation and technical reporting by converting implementation work into a structured workshop.

**5. Program structure and communication**

The program gave me enough freedom to explore, but that also meant the quality of my direction depended heavily on how early I could narrow the project. Looking back, I think clearer early examples of workshop scope, stronger reminders to reduce exploration drift, and more checkpoints focused on reviewer-facing output would have helped me converge faster. The communication structure was still useful, but I benefited most when expectations became concrete.

**6. Internship process / expectations**

The process became much clearer near the end, when the project was framed not only as an implementation task but also as a workshop submission with validation evidence, evaluation notes, and cleanup guidance. Earlier in the internship, I spent more time learning and experimenting than building the final submission directly. That made the journey longer, but it also gave the final workshop a stronger technical foundation than a purely narrow implementation path would have provided.

## Additional questions

**What did you find most satisfying during your internship?**

The most satisfying part was having the opportunity to work directly with AWS services in a real project. Although I had wanted to spend more time on that earlier, I had not been able to do so before this internship. The internship gave me a concrete reason and a practical setting to learn AWS through implementation rather than only through theory, which made the experience especially meaningful to me.

**What do you think should be improved for future interns?**

I think future interns would benefit from earlier help with project scoping and stronger examples of what a good final workshop repo actually looks like. It would also help to clarify earlier that technical exploration is useful, but the final submission still needs a narrow, reviewer-facing architecture, visible evidence, and cleanup discipline. More structured checkpoints on those points could save a lot of drift.

**If recommending this program to a friend, would you suggest it? Why or why not?**

Yes, I would recommend it, especially to university students who want to grow beyond classroom-style knowledge and learn how technical work becomes a practical deliverable. I think the experience is particularly suitable for students who have enough free time to commit seriously to the process, for example after completing most required coursework. For students who are already balancing a major thesis or several heavy responsibilities at the same time, the experience can still be valuable, but it may be harder to benefit from it fully without enough time and focus.

## Suggestions and expectations

- Help interns narrow project scope earlier.
- Show good final repo examples earlier.
- Add a few structured checkpoints during the internship so project direction can be reviewed before the final submission stage.
- Make the final submission expectations clearer earlier in the program.
